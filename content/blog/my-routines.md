Routines are how I control my life.  Trust me when I say this, I'm not one of those junkies that gets up, drinks a gallon of water, does yoga for an hour then eats an avocado toast with handfuls of supplements.  I'm not that guy.  And that's not this post.

But I am pretty disciplined about a few things.  At least, for most of the year.  However, because I can rely on routines I've built over the years, it allows me to pivot off of them for a period of time without regret.  I know I can go back to the routine because it's durable and built for the long haul.  That's what I want to share with you.  Hope some of this helps.

## The Routine Stack
My routines are categorized into daily and annual.  Daily routines are just that -- what I do every day.  Annual routines are longer-term and always start on January 1st.  No exceptions for either.  This goes whether I'm working or on a [work pause](https://jonnywren.com/blog/work-pause/).

The one thing that may truly annoy you is that I am a morning person.  Always have been as far as I can remember.  I get bored sleeping.  I know I need it but, candidly, I wish I didn't.  I like doing stuff -- and that requires me to be awake.  I know that's not everyone's cup of tea but the timing is irrelevant.  Read with your own perspective.

### Daily Routine,  Part 1:  Hit the floor, then to-do list
1. Get out of bed, no later than 6am:  *Here's the annoying thing, I'm usually out of bed by 4:30am.*
2. Make that pot of coffee
3. Simple stretch:  Not full on yoga stuff, just a couple of stretches that force me to the carpet.  *Hitting the floor is the important part*.  Because it actually gets some blood moving, not just pulling a leg back leaning against a wall.
4. Check the weather:  This determines a LOT for how I conduct my day
5. Review To-Do list:  My favorite part of the morning
6. Journal and track my calories:  End of day routine

Between 5am and when the kids get up, that's my most "alert" time of the day.  My brain got rest and is usually at it's most creative point of the day.  I take advantage of that.

New ideas, new projects, travel plans, calendar planning -- all happens during this timeframe.  For us "agile-planners" this is where I review, edit, and update my backlog.  This includes anything from writing new blog posts, repotting garden starts, and making dinner for the family.  Also, pending the weather, determines where I will work.  Again, currently on a work-pause, I can just work from my home office if I want.  But, that home office is also my music studio...all my gear is in there.  It's very easy to get sidetracked, especially if something on my to-do list for the day is "learn that new song".  So, i hit up libraries as they are free, quiet, and creates a productive setting.  I love libraries.

To summarize:  I get up, make coffee, quick stretch (on the floor), check weather, the hit the to-do list.  Once I feel like I've got a solid to-do list, then I consider that the **start** of my day.  After, I'll shower, eat breakfasst, etc etc.

### Daily Routine,  Part 2: Reassess the to-do list
Yep, after all that, I look at my list and say "there's no way all that's all going to happen"

I had just spent an hour setting it up perfectly.  Then, I immediately cherry pick the one or two things that I actually will do that day.  Why?  Because I know the day will not be perfect.  Not even close.

I set up my to-do list with such vigor in the hopes that I'll have the most productive day ever.  The reality is, only about 20% of that plan ever gets done.  Sometimes, none of it gets done.  I'll roll it all over to the next day.

But, here's the takeaway:  **Just the act of reviewing and editing the to-do list creates me a document of all the things I want to do in life.**

Everything from "make chicken for dinner" to "plan the bike trip in Germany" -- all of it goes on the same list.  I don't worry about order.  Just "if I had a utopian day of productivity, this is what want to do".  Hardly ever do I get a day where I get to do everything I planned.

But, again, not the point.  The point is to update my list.  Think of it as drinking a beer but not finishing it.  I pour the remaining in a large jar for later.  That *jar of swill* is my bucket list.  And everything goes in there.

The next day, I'll review it all and start all over.  **My list never gets done.  The point is, the list keeps growing.**  

Because of that, so will I.

I'll pick a few things off the list and commit.  Then, shower, eat, and go.

Whether you do this at 5am or 10pm doesn't matter.  Add to your jar of swill!

## Annual Routines
I have two, both are health related.

1. Target Weight
2. Dry January and February

That's it.  And, that keeps me wicked healthy for the majority of the year.  But, it also allows me to let myself go as I'll explain.

### Target Weight
Wherever I start on January 1st is what it is.  The target weight is always the same.  For me, it's 155#.  I have to hit that, however I do it, sometime in the year.  Ideally, the sooner the better but there is no specific date required.  It's just somewhere during the year.  And, because of that, delaying it can actually make me "healthier" for longer periods during the year.

For example, if I set a goal of 155 for March, and hit it, then I feel like I accomplished my goal.  And, if I want, can let myself go. But, if I don't, then the process by which of reaching that goal lingers for longer in the year -- and that's ok!  Because, my routine is still executing.  I'm eating healthier, working out, and counting my calories -- for a longer duration in the year.  I might not be at 155 but plateauing at 160 ain't bad.  Since I'm not *done* I'm still in routine mode.  That's a good thing.

Honestly, when I do hit 155, I feel like I've accomplished one of my annual routines.  Check.  Done.  Great.  Go have a piece of blackberry pie.  Maybe 3.  I can reward myself now.  

Here's the takeaway:  **I can rely on myself to do it again next year because it's a durable routine.**  

For the rest of my life, I'll hit 155# somewhere in that year.  End stop.  As long as I stay disciplined in that, I can indulge as I wish.

### Dry January and February
Same story here.  No booze for the first two months of the year, period.  But, after doing this for a few years, something surprised me.

I started this routine when I turned 41.  I was your typical drinker:  come home from work, pour a good measure of spirit, toss in a piece of ice, and enjoy.  I never really enjoyed beer, mostly spirits.   My dad was this way so I was just following in his footsteps.  Nothing wrong with that, right?

Well, why?  It wasn't adding any value, the measures got heftier and heftier, and the weight pilled on.  Plus, like my dad, I have gout [link to gout webmd post].  Alcohol only makes that worse.

So I started this routine.  I didn't want to be like my dad in this case.  He had long since passed of Pancreatic Cancer [another link here] so I didn't have to explain my rational to him.  And, maybe that was the reason behind his cancer.

Originally, it was just "Dryuary" -- January only.  But, when I got to the end of the month, I felt so good that I decided to keep going through February.  I remember that March 1st -- I was in California visiting a buddy for a surf outing and finally had my first drink in two months.  But, I also hit my target weight by then as well.  So, why not.  We had a good time, I attempted to surf in the freezing cold water, and life kinda went back to the way it was before January.

As expected, the weight pilled on and old habits came back.  Ok, that's fine.  But, I decided at that point:  I'm never going to have any alcohol in January and February ever again.  10 years later, it's held up.

But, here's the surprising thing I teased you with:  I barely drink anymore.

Because, every year I executed this routine, the duration got longer.  The second year, I went all the way to Easter (which was late in April).  A year or two later, I started it early and decided to take "all of age 44 off" --  and did.  **Every year, the routine became more and more durable.**. I can confidently rely on it and, thus, can indulge if I choose.

Now in my 50s, I still do the routine but it's almost meaningless due to the reduction of alcohol intake.  What was once "most of the year" now is "only when I'm on a road trip" which is rare.  I've got 4 kids, I don't get many road trips.  But, when I do, I don't have regrets.  And, that's not a routine:  "Have booze on road-trip".  It's just an option, if I choose.

However, I still commit to the routine but it automatically extends by itself.  It's that strong now, but it took me many years of execution. I didn't plan that, it just happened all by itself.  And, I'm totally fine with it.

Look, I get that might not work for everyone.  Some people have more dependency that others and I'm not advocating for any one of them to "try this instead of that".  It's my routine and works for me.  **The point is, after many years of execution, it's durable and reliable.**

Be patient, truly durable routines won't happen overnight.


## When A Routine Is...Done
Wha?  You just set me up to make a bunch of routines that should do forever.

No I didn't.  I just stated what my routines are.  What I didn't state was routines that are no longer routines.

Working out, for example.  

Back in high school, I wanted to make the lacrosse team.  But I started late.  Most players had played since, at least, freshman year.  I started just before my Junior year in high school.  That year, I was a goalie on the JV team.  It didn't require any physical ability other than the insanity of standing in goal and stopping 90mph rocks hurled at me.  The following year, I was trying out for Varsity.  But, our goalie was All American, no way I was getting playing time.  So, I tried for different position -- which did require a higher level of physicality.  I'm only 5'7'',  so I needed to put on some pounds.

I started to hit the gym, regularly.  I was about 15 or 16 at the time, can't remember exactly.  But, getting on 40 years later, I still do it.  Not every day, and definitely not like when I was younger.  It has taken it's toll over time.  I popped my shoulder mid bench-press once, that was fun.  The shoulder has never really healed since, but it works good enough.

I used to track my workouts -- ex: how many times I benched 215#, dumbbell curls, squats, etc etc.  I had a routine back then -- every night, not matter what, hit the gym.  My goal was to eventually bench 300# (which, never happened, FYI)

None of that is tracked anymore.  Why?  Not because I don't go to gym anymore -- I still do.  In fact, when I take too much time off, my body knows something's amiss.  Almost subconsciously, it goes in to the gym by itself for some kind of work out regardless of what my brain is thinking.  It's automatic.

It's a lifestyle.

I don't track my breathing.  And, I almost consider it the same.  Somewhere along the way, the routine turned into a lifestyle.  I don't know exactly when, it just happened.  It's so durable that it's no longer worth tracking.  It's done.

Now, I use it as a tool to get me to other goals that are part of my current routines.  Example, my target weight.  The gym is a handy tool but it's not the only tool.  The only think I track is the output of that tool for my current routines.  How many calories did I burn -- that's directly related to my target weight routine.  Write it down.

The shoulder is not going to let me bench anymore. I don't care, I'm not playing ball anymore.  But, I'm sure glad that I started that routine way back when.

## Where To Start
If you have no routines, or none that you consider durable, I have one for you.  And, it's stupidly simple.

Count your calories

I didn't start this one until sometime last year (2025).  I'm getting older.  I have a bunch of kids.  Time is getting more and more limited and capacity is not coming back anytime soon.  In order for me to reach my target goal every year, I can't afford to kill it in the gym for 2 hours a night.  Nor do I want to.  I like to be around my family.

That's what I used to think:  get to your target goal by busting ass in the gym.  And, it works.  Since it's a lifestyle for me, it's not a foreign concept.  I've been doing it for decades.  But, nothing is forever.  And, how I spend my time is changing.  I needed to find another way.

Using AI, I built myself a personal journal which includes a calorie tracker.  Call it what it is: it's a glorified diary.  In fact, my GitHub repo is still labeled "diary" even though the app says "Personal Journal".  It's all local, on my machines.  I don't like using online services for my personal information.  Something makes me feel sketchy about it so I built my own. [Link to this future post]

For Part 1 of my daily routine, I've been journaling and recording my caloric intake and burn.  This creates me my net caloric intake which determines whether I've been a good boy or bad.  I also record how my day went, the things I accomplished, and a general score myself for the day. All text, no "drop downs", no "swiping", just text.  It doesn't have any "meal plan" in there, it's all retrospective and estimated.  What did I do that day.

What has fascinated me is that, in just a few months, that routine has fundamentally affected my lifestyle. It forces me to read labels, portion control, and choose the best options when it comes to food.  I know that 3500 calories is 1lb.  So, creating myself that deficiency, I'll lose a pound.  It's not diet, it's physics.  So my metric is "Caloric Deficiency".  Again, it's estimated --  I don't weigh my food, just eyeball it.  "That's about 300 calories".  When I don't know, I look it up.  I don't know how many times I've looked up how many calories are in one fun-size Almond Joy because I keep forgetting.  (Or, I purposely forget...so I can have one....or two)

Because I'm tracking my caloric deficiency, I eat less.  Combine that with my *hardened workout lifestyle* I burn what I need to for the day to create some deficiency.  If it's 500 calories, great.  If it's 1000 calories, even better.  If it's nothing, ok, at least I know that.

**I don't need to hammer in the gym anymore!**.  If I feel I have enough deficiency for the day, I'm done.  Before, I would track and ensure that I burnt my 600 calories for the day.  Now, with this little app I created for myself via AI,  I can get all that time back by simply by calculating my caloric deficiency.

And, man does it work!!  I've been tracking this routine for about a decade so I have my historical data for how I got there in the past.  This is the first year that I've reached my goal -- with the LEAST amount of time in the gym.  You read that right -- I got to the same spot with hardly half the effort.

All because of my little app that I built for myself.  Customized for me, how I think, and my routines.  Tracking the *right metrics.*  Committing to spending a few minutes per day to journal and read some labels.  If you had to choose between "a few minutes a day" versus "a few hours a day", which one would you choose??  No brainer.
