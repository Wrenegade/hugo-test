## 1. AI Patent Trolling — Who Owns the Idea You Haven't Had Yet?

**The setup:**  You sit down with AI, brainstorm the next big thing — a product, a process, whatever.  You're excited.  You call your buddy to pitch it.  Too late.  Someone already filed the patent.

**How it works:**  Legally, AI can't be named as an inventor.  The USPTO confirmed this in November 2025 — AI is just a "tool," like a calculator.  Only humans get inventor credit.  Sounds like protection, right?

It's the opposite.  Here's what it actually enables:

- Someone uses AI to generate **thousands** of patentable variations on ideas — broadly worded, thinly plausible
- They put **their name** on all of them as the "inventor" (because the human who prompted the AI gets the credit)
- AI has made writing patent applications **trivially cheap** — what used to take a team of lawyers now takes an afternoon
- They sit on a **minefield** of patents and wait for someone to independently come up with one of those ideas
- Then they sue

**It's already happening:**
- Patent trolls (non-practicing entities) account for **more than half** of all AI-related patent lawsuits
- **1 in 5** NPE lawsuits in tech is now AI-related
- Startups are **twice as likely** to be targeted because trolls know they can't afford to fight
- The cost of mass-filing patents with AI is dropping to near zero while the cost of defending against them stays sky-high

**The T&Cs problem:**  If you're using a proprietary AI tool to brainstorm, read the fine print.  If the terms say they retain rights to outputs or derivative works... good luck arguing ownership in court.  There's no precedent.  The case law is being written right now — with your ideas as the test cases.

**The gut punch:**  The barrier to *creating* ideas dropped to zero.  But the barrier to *owning* them just got weaponized.  The 10,000 who understand the system will file the patents.  Everyone else will independently "invent" something that's already been claimed on paper by someone who never built it.

## 2. AI Is Supercharging Elder Scams — And It's Already a $5 Billion Problem

**This one's personal.**  I lost my mom to Jamaican lottery scammers.  Not figuratively — they killed her.  Six years of false hope, fear, isolation, and financial destruction that ended with her dying in a hospital in Stockton, California with a hole in her stomach.  I wrote the [full story here](/blog/jamaican-lottery-scams-and-my-mom/).

That was 2018.  The scammers who got my mom used burner phones, fake IRS documents, and hollowed-out books for laundering cash.  Low tech.  It still took $250K and her life.

Now imagine that operation with AI.

**The numbers today (without AI fully deployed):**
- **$4.9 billion** lost by Americans 60+ to fraud in 2024 (FBI IC3 Report)
- **147,127** elder fraud complaints filed — up **46%** from 2023
- Average loss per victim: **$83,000** — up **43%** year over year
- Total elder fraud losses have gone up **4x since 2020** — from $600M to $2.4B in FTC-reported losses alone
- First quarter of 2025: **$745 million** lost already
- Investment scams led with **$1.8 billion** in losses for 60+ victims
- And these are just the *reported* numbers.  Most elderly victims never report — they don't know how, they're embarrassed, or they don't even know they've been scammed

**Now add AI to the mix:**
- AI can clone a person's voice with **85% accuracy using just 3 seconds of audio**.  Three seconds.  A voicemail greeting.  A TikTok your grandkid posted
- **1 in 4 Americans** has already received an AI-generated deepfake voice call
- **77%** of victims who engaged with an AI scam call lost money
- The "grandparent scam" — where someone calls pretending to be a grandchild in trouble — just went from a bad impersonation to an **exact replica of your grandchild's voice** saying "Grandma, I'm scared, please don't tell Mom and Dad"
- The FBI issued a warning in 2025 about AI-simulated kidnappings demanding ransoms of $2,500 to $15,000
- Scammers now scrape social media for details — vacation photos, pet names, check-in locations — and weave them into the call.  It's not generic anymore.  It's *personalized*

**What my mom's scammers did with phone calls and fake documents, AI does at 1000x the speed, 1000x the scale, for near-zero cost.**

The protection racket from the patent trolling entry above?  Same model.  Create the fear (your grandchild is in danger), sell the solution (send money now).  Oldest business model in the world, now running on the most powerful technology ever built, aimed at the most vulnerable people in society.

**What you can do:**
- Set up a family code word — something only your family knows, spoken before any money moves
- Tell your elderly relatives: **if anyone calls asking for money and says "don't tell anyone," hang up and call the family member directly**
- Report to the FBI's IC3 at ic3.gov and the FTC at reportfraud.ftc.gov

My mom didn't have anyone telling her this.  By the time I found out, it was too late.  Don't let that be your family.
