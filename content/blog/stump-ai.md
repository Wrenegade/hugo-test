My morning question to AI.  "What does the labor force look like in 5, 10, 15, 20 years — and where do I point my kids?"

Down that rabbit hole I went.  90 minutes later, this is how that wrestling match went.

> **AI:** *"That's who you're really writing for.  Not the 10,000.  Not the plumber.  The person in between with nothing to grab onto.  And I don't have a clean answer for that.  Which maybe is exactly where you are too.  Still stuck on the same question, just sharper now."*

> **Me:** *"Great, I've stumped you too.  I'm on the right track.  This makes me feel better.  Because now, you truly are a thought partner.  And whatever we do from here is exactly what they are going to do in the future.  Get it now?!?!"*

I pinned AI!  And my next question, which I said to myself was "where do I go from here?"  Because, it no longer had answers for me.  I was on my own.

## How We Got Here

It started a few days ago with an analysis I was doing on the American labor force.  Simply out of curiosity but, also, with my kids in the back of my mind.  Who would they compete with when that day comes?  So, I grabbed my normal set of stats that frames it up for me.

| Category | Count |
|---|---|
| Total American Population (16+) | 275 million |
| Total Labor Force (working or actively looking) | 170.5 million |
| Total Not In Labor Force | 104.5 million |
| **Total** | **275 million** |

Of the 170.5 million in the labor force:

| Status | Count |
|---|---|
| Employed | 163 million |
| Unemployed (actively looking) | 7.5 million |
| **Total** | **170.5 million** |

That's how you get the unemployment rate: 7.5 / 170.5 = 4.4%.  About what I see --  on TikTok.

Now, the 104.5 million who are *out* of the labor force entirely:

| Category | Count |
|---|---|
| Retired (I'm not working anymore) | 47.5 million |
| Disabled (I can't work anymore) | 14.7 million |
| Stay at home (Caring for others) | 12.7 million |
| Students (full time) | 15 million |
| Other (incarcerated, institutionalized, gig/under the table, or checked out) | 14.6 million |
| **Total** | **104.5 million** |

These people don't show up in the unemployment number at all.  As far as the government is concerned, they don't exist in the workforce conversation.

So, what was the historical low for unemployment?  In 2000, it was 4%.  That's considered "full employment".  During the Great Depression, it was 25%.  COVID actually peaked it up to almost 15%.  The financial crisis hit 9%.  [Here's the full picture since 1948.](/data/unemployment_rate_history.html)

Ok, so we're not in bad shape.  Kids should be ok.  But, why does it not feel like that?

*Aaaaaaand that's where this rabbit hole opened up.*

## The Numbers Under The Rug
Ok, unemployment is low and income is at an all-time high.  But, AI kept telling me the sentiment score is down.

Sentiment score?  Well, I didn't know it either, until just now.

The **University of Michigan Consumer Sentiment Index** asks the same 5 questions to a random sample of 500 households every month.  Been doing it since 1966.
- Are you better or worse off financially than a year ago?
- Will you be better or worse off a year from now?
- Is now a good or bad time to buy major household items?
- How will business conditions be in the next 12 months?
- How will business conditions be in the next 5 years?

I knew about the Consumer Confidence index which tracks more business outlooky stuff.  That's been falling too but I didn't know about the sentiment score.  Soooo....how's that going?

Same story, it's falling.  Yet, again, unemployment is low *and* income is high -- according to the BLS and BEA numbers that all the politicians talk about. 

Back in 2000, everything was at the "best" levels we've seen in our lifetime (that's you, if you are alive and reading this).  Income high, unemployment low -> high confidence and high sentiment.  Makes sense.  Using that as a benchmark against today, you'd think the numbers would be similar -- but they're not.

| Metric | 2000 | 2026 |
|---|---|---|
| Unemployment | 4.0% | 4.4% |
| Income | High | Higher |
| Consumer Sentiment | 107.6 | ~65 |
| Consumer Confidence | 144.7 | ~91 |

Income *is* high, unemployment *is* low -- and sentiment *is low*.
In fact, it's dropped 40% since 2000.  Technically, people are getting richer *and* more miserable at the same time.  *"Mo Money, Mo Problems"* might be the new national anthem soon.

So, I kept going.  

I found out that, since 2000, [labor force participation](/data/labor-participation/) has dropped from 67.3% to 62.0% — that's roughly 14.6 million fewer workers than our peak.  Ok, so how many of them would have just aged out naturally?  Turns out, roughly half.  So, of the 14.6 that left, let's say 7.6 of them are now out of the game for good.

But, that still leaves 7 million qualified workers who just totally bailed.  

Wait wait,  hold it --  We currently have 7ish million people looking for jobs (which is on par) and had 7 million give up over the last decade.  Yet unemployment is low and income is high*er* -- feels like a denominator is off.  Oh yeah, and sentiment and confidence is down.  So, "You people, who are all working and making fat stacks, are unhappy and less confident?"

It just wasn't adding up for me.  What am I supposed to tell my kids?  "You'll be fine.  Look, unemployment rate...income rate....you're set!"  Yeah, but...

## So who are these 7 million?

I wanted to know who actually left and why.  The 7 million who *could* be working but are choosing not to for some reason.

| Group | Count | Avg Age |
|---|---|---|
| Disabled | ~1.5M | ~56 |
| Caregivers | ~2M | ~35 |
| Disgruntled | ~1.5M | ~45 |
| Pushed Out | ~1.2M | ~60 |
| Severe | ~800K | ~40 |
| **Total** | **~7M** | **~47** |

- **Disabled (~1.5M, avg age ~56):** These people have conditions that qualify for SSDI and receive a monthly check from the Feds.  Under the right conditions, there could be decent opportunities in the remote workforce for many.  But, here's the catch:  if they make over a certain amount then they no longer qualify for SSDI.  So, small paycheck for life or risk it.
- **Caregivers (~2M, avg age ~35):** Mostly women, very qualified, and would make less than what childcare costs.  They would *lose* money by working.  The math just doesn't make sense for them.
- **Disgruntled (~1.5M, avg age ~45):** People who have been searching hard with no luck.  At some point, it's reasonable to say "what's the point" so they stop searching. 
- **Pushed Out (~1.2M, avg age ~60):** People in their late 50s and early 60s who got pushed out during COVID layoffs.  Some took early Social Security at 62 and locked in a reduced benefit for life.  The rest live off savings until that runs out.
- **Severe** (~800K, avg age ~40): Society has rejected them culturally and/or deemed them too risky.  Either the consistent cycle of poverty and/or drugs creating negative hope.  Entire towns — Marion, Ohio; Bruceton, Tennessee; half of rural West Virginia — factories closed, pills showed up, there goes a generation.

These people don't show up anywhere on jobs reports.  They're not looking, so they're not "unemployed".  Add that number back into the mix and you're looking at 8.5% unemployment (14.5 / 170.5).  That's a big difference.  If I was a politician, I would totally sweep that under the rug.

The weighted average age across all 7 million is 47 years old.  They have 20 years of working life ahead of them but the system headwinds keep them on the sidelines.  There are incentives for the private industry to create opportunities for them but it's laughable.  $1,500 tax break if they can create an opportunity for SSDI.  For big business, acquisition costs run around $4,000 per employee.  After the administration costs to recoup a lousy $1,500, they might stand to make a few hundred bucks.  Not worth it, so no one does it.

## Which 7 Million Pool Will My Kids Be In?
When starting a career, everyone falls into the first 7 million pool.  Actively looking, hopes high, dreams big.  It's a few decades later that your spirits get crushed by the weight of the world.

And, I hope it's a few "decades" before that happens.  My fear is will it be only a few "years".  The young, idealistic kids that think they can solve the world's problems -- we need that.  I don't need to be telling them that's never going to happen.  They need to figure that out for themselves.  Oh, it'll happen, but hopefully after they've established themselves in a system that works for them.

And the system we have today isn't going to be there for my two littles.  **So, what kind of system do I think will be waiting for them?**

That's the 10 million dollar question.  And, where I brought AI to its knees.  We riffed.

The first round of answers was predictable.  AI review roles, human needed roles (doctors, therapists, plumbers).  We riffed on how the gig work industry could thrive by creating millions of small task-oriented jobs.  Tagging data for AI models....that market already exists today.  Lots of ideas on how to generate multiple streams of income assuming most of the system will be automated.

I liked it.  For about 10 minutes.

Because that's just another dashboard metric.  "We created 500,000 gig positions!"  Great.  Are people *happy*?  Do they feel like they're *building* something?  Or did we just invent a new way to make the unemployment number look better while people tag images for $8 an hour from their couch.

## The Problem with Jobs

I don't want my kids to just have a job, or jobs.  I want them to have a purpose.  A reason to want to get up and challenge themselves.

I have four kids.  My oldest daughter (14) wants to be a cop.  Probably AI proof, done.  My son (12) is a quiet savant — he'll end up building things, probably brilliant (and dangerous) ones.  We're looking at 10 years before he gets an opportunity to build a new and better bomb.  I think he'll be fine but we'll see.

But my two youngest daughters — 5 and 7.  They'll enter the workforce around 2039 to 2043.  By that time, everything will have shifted.  And this is the generation I'm worried about -- because I got two of them.

I asked AI to give me three future jobs.  It gave me "AI System Shepherd" and "Trust Auditor" and "Micro-Economy Designer."  Nobody dreams about being a shepherd.  Nobody's 7-year-old draws a picture of themselves auditing.  Those are *tasks*, not *purposes*.

So I started throwing industries at AI to see if anything held up.

## Where I Stumped AI

What about clothing?  AI can design it, a system can sew it, robots will ship it.  Nope.  What about farming?  Hydroponics, vertical gardening -- 3 people will be able to manage a thousand acres.  What about child care?  Ok, maybe.  There will always be demand for that as I wouldn't trust a robot with my kid.  What do they get paid, again?  Oh right...

F' that.

"But gig work can supplement their income."

F' that too!

I don't want them working 3 jobs just to pay the rent.  Besides, no one is having kids anymore so that industry is collapsing.  [Have you seen the birth rates lately?](/data/birth-rate/) I sincerely hope that's economic related ("I can't afford it") versus emotional ("I don't want to bring a kid into this world").  Might be related to sentiment decline?  Hmm...

Every path led to the same place: AI compresses everything *digital* to near zero.  Design, code, content, analysis — all of it collapses to maybe 10,000 people worldwide running the whole shop.  But it can't compress *raw materials*.  Cotton still needs to grow.  Water still needs to flow.  The pipe still leaks.  The school still needs a human in the room for, at least, legalities. 

So the future economy splits between **digital and physical**.

The digital side will be controlled by 10K people worldwide.  The physical side — plumbers, electricians, cops, nurses — that's probably safe because AI can't show up.

But what about the person in between?  Customer service, marketing, office managers -- living in an apartment, no land to leverage, no trade skills -- but they still have a purpose.  There's a lot of those people.  What happens to them?

That's where I stumped AI.  It didn't have an answer.

## And That's the Point
After patting myself on the back for successfully getting AI to say, essentially, "I don't know" here's what I realized.  That was my answer.  Now, I have a new sandbox to play in, one that AI doesn't know about.  And it may take years to catch up.

For 90 minutes, I pushed it hard through labor economics, gig marketplaces, education policy, manufacturing, agriculture, clothing design — until it ran out of road.  I got to a topic that was *new* to explore.  Not because I'm smarter than it but because I was stubborn enough to keep asking "yeah, but *why*" until the machine shrugged.  There was no data it could fall back on because I was talking about things that are yet to come.

It's like a new guitar riff combined with an attitude that suddenly creates a whole new genre of music.  "Punk AI" -- hey, that's good.  Let me see if that domain is available.....nope.  [Some asshole parked it.](https://punkai.com)  I f'ing hate that!  Now how am I going to use "Never Mind the *AI* Bollocks, Here's My Kids!"?

But you get the point.  Remember, back in the day, when indie music was cool because you and three others were the only ones that knew about it?  Same concept, and I think those days are coming back.

And that's what I plan to teach my kids.  Not "learn to code" or "get a trade" or "be an AI shepherd."

**Push the machine to the edge of what it knows.  Then, start there.  Be the punks of AI**

Because everything before that will get automated by someone in that pool of 10,000.  That's the thing that's already been thought of -- or currently being trained to.  There's no chance of winning there especially in 5 years, no more than 10.

The *human* value lives past that edge where the machine can't answer.  And it only took me 90 minutes to find one of them.  Now I can clearly see an opportunity.

## For My Kids
I think, as of today, the train is moving slow enough where my two oldest (14 and 12) can hop on and ride it to someplace.  It won't be luxurious but, hopefully, sustainable.  However, they may need help from mommy and daddy at some point so I'll prepare for that now.

For my two youngest (5 and 7) that train will be long gone by the time they hit that station.  What the vehicle will be, no clue.  So, I'm going to plan that it probably won't even exist or, probably, be some autonomous bus.  Which, in turn, frames my thinking *now*.

Honestly, I still don't know how to point them.  Besides, they're too young anyway.  But, in the coming years, I'm going to keep my eyes open for those opportunities I mentioned.  "Let's ask AI, my little love love" then "Are you sure about that answer?  Why?" -- with gentle nudges.

Over time, that will evolve to "I bet you can't stump it" or "$20 if you can stump it".  Seriously, provide monetary incentive.  Why not?  Anything to motivate them to keep going and find out where AI can't provide a reasonable answer anymore.  Honestly, I'd much rather spend the college tuition savings like that versus handing it over to an aging University infrastructure (that's a whole other post).  I can guarantee that I'll be the happiest daddy in the world when one of them says "HA!  I got it!  It doesn't know."

Because that's where their opportunity starts.  

After that, who the hell knows.
