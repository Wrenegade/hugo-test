## What You're Looking At

The U.S. crude birth rate — live births per 1,000 people — shaded by the generation being born during each era.

The Baby Boom is impossible to miss.  Post-war optimism, [the GI Bill](/fact_check_timeline.html#1944-gi-bill-signed-servicemen-s-readjustment-act), cheap housing, and single-income families that could afford four kids drove the rate above 25 per 1,000.  Then [the pill arrived](/fact_check_timeline.html#1960-fda-approves-the-birth-control-pill), women entered the workforce, and it fell off a cliff.

Every generation since has had fewer kids than the one before.  Gen X was born during the trough.  Millennials arrived during a brief rebound that never got close to Boomer levels.  Gen Z was born as the rate crossed below the replacement threshold (~13.5 per 1,000) — and Gen Alpha is being born into the lowest birth rates in American history.

The red dashed line marks the approximate replacement rate — the birth rate needed to maintain population without immigration.  The U.S. has been below it since roughly 2008.

**The key takeaway:**  Fewer people being born means fewer future workers, fewer taxpayers, and more pressure on the ones who remain.  This chart is the other half of the labor force story.
